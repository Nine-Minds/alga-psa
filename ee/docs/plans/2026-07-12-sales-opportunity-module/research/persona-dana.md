# Persona interview: Dana — new solo MSP owner (ex-credit-union IT)

> Design-research artifact for the sales opportunity module. Simulated persona interview, 2026-07-12.
> Persona: solo MSP, ~20 months in, ~14 clients (credit-union/community-bank niche + word-of-mouth SMBs), ~$22k MRR, one part-time tech. Excellent at delivery and compliance; hates selling; drops deals via emotional avoidance. Tried and abandoned HubSpot free.

## 1. Last three wins, two losses

Win one: Pinewood FCU, about $58M in assets. Their compliance officer got dinged on an NCUA exam for vendor management and IT oversight, and a compliance officer at another credit union I serve gave her my name. The whole deal was seven emails and one on-site. The thread lived in Gmail under a star. Honestly they chased *me* — she emailed twice when I went quiet for nine days after sending the proposal (a Word doc I rebuilt from scratch, again). $2,900/mo. I did nothing right except be the person who could say "FFIEC CAT" without flinching.

Win two: a dental practice I'd been break-fixing. The office next door to them got hit with ransomware, and the office manager grabbed my arm during a visit and said "can that happen to us?" I quoted $1,450/mo verbally, standing in their hallway, and she said yes before I finished. I know I undercharged by probably $400 because I panicked and picked a number that wouldn't make her face change. No thread anywhere — the "contract" started as a text message.

Win three: Harvest Community Bank. They paid me $4,500 for an IT/security assessment — that part I sold easily, because an assessment doesn't feel like selling, it feels like an audit, which is my native language. My findings doc had 23 items. Six months later their one IT guy quit and they called me. The findings doc *was* the proposal; I just had to not screw up the call. $3,800/mo.

Loss one: a three-branch credit union, ~$120M. Real opportunity, warm intro. I sent a proposal; they replied with four questions, one of which was "can you break pricing into tiers by branch?" I didn't have tiers. That email sat marked-unread for sixteen days while I told myself I was "thinking about pricing." They signed with a regional MSP. The story I told myself: "they were always going to pick the bigger firm." Maybe. But I'll never know, because functionally I ghosted them.

Loss two: Gary, a 25-seat machine shop. Chamber event, he said "send me a quote." It lived nowhere — my head, plus an Apple Note that literally says "quote for Gary??" No date, no dollar amount. I saw him at a chamber lunch four months later and we both pretended it hadn't happened. That one wasn't lost, it just... evaporated.

## 2. What dropping the ball physically looks like

Logistically: an email marked unread for the third time. A draft that's 80% written sitting in Drafts for eleven days. A note with no date. There is no list of open deals anywhere — so nothing ever *tells* me I'm late; I just occasionally remember at 11pm.

Emotionally, it's a compounding shame loop. Day 3, the follow-up is easy. Day 10, the follow-up now has to open with an apology, which makes it heavier, which makes me avoid it more. Day 20, replying feels more humiliating than losing the deal, so I let it die and reclassify it as "wasn't a fit." The logistics problem is trivial — I run patch schedules for six financial institutions, I clearly can operate a reminder. The problem is I've built a system where nothing external forces the moment, so my avoidance always wins.

## 3. What I track today, and HubSpot

Today: Gmail stars, one Apple Note called "Pipeline??" with maybe six lines on it, and my head. That's it. I can tell you the renewal date of every client's core banking vendor contract, and I cannot tell you how many open quotes I have.

HubSpot: it asked me to define deal stages, lifecycle stages, and log activities before it did anything for me. That's vocabulary for a sales *team* — someone managing reps. I'm not managing reps, I'm managing my own flinch. Every login was an empty dashboard implying I'd failed at homework. Three weeks, out. Also nothing in it knew anything about my actual business — my clients, my tickets, my invoices all lived elsewhere, so it was a second, emptier world.

## 4. New logos vs. existing clients

New logos come from referrals and exam findings — compliance officers talk to each other, and that's basically my whole funnel. Maybe 3-4 real at-bats a year.

Existing clients are where I don't even see it as sales, which is both good and bad. Good: when I tell Harvest they need MFA on their VPN or a backup change, I say it like an engineer and they just do it. There's no fraud feeling because I'm not "selling," I'm "recommending." Bad: because it's not sales in my head, it's not systematic. I have never once swept all 14 clients and asked "who doesn't have the security stack, who's on aging hardware, who's break-fix that should be on an agreement?" I know for a fact two clients are running 6-year-old servers and I haven't quoted refreshes because the quotes are annoying to build and nobody's asking. That's probably $30-40k of project work sitting in my own client list that I'd close at 80% if I just... surfaced it.

## 5. First two weeks of an "opportunities" area

Keep me: it has to start full, not empty. It already has my clients, contacts, tickets, and billing — so on day one it should say "here are 4 threads in your email that look like open deals" and "here are 3 clients on T&M who spent more than an agreement would cost." Then, one next action per deal with a date, shoved in my face where I already live — my ticket queue or a Monday digest. Not a dashboard. A short list of "do this today."

Abandon it: any required field ceremony (stages, probabilities, forecast categories), any big empty funnel visualization judging me, or any double entry — if I have to log an email I already sent, it's dead. If opening it feels like opening HubSpot did — a mirror of my neglect — I'll stop opening it within ten days, guaranteed.

## 6. Automation acting for me

Trust without asking: reminders, obviously. Drafting follow-ups — yes, please, because the blank compose window is where I die; a decent draft turns a 20-minute dread task into a 90-second edit. Auto-*sending*: for the low-stakes "just checking in on that quote" class, to a machine shop, after I've approved the first couple — probably yes, eventually. For credit unions, never without review; tone with a compliance officer matters and a wrong note costs me the referral network, which is my entire pipeline.

Escalating stale deals — it's just me, so "escalate" means *interrupt*, not notify. Put the stale deal in front of me at a moment I can't scroll past, like blocking 20 minutes on my calendar with the draft pre-written. Escalating to my part-time tech, no — he's a tech, and I'd be embarrassed anyway.

Creepy line: telling a prospect "I saw you opened my proposal Tuesday" — never. More than two unanswered nudges. Anything that sounds like a sequence — my clients are bankers, they get vendor spam all day and they can smell it.

## 7. MSP mechanics I fumble

Assessments: my best move and I've never productized it — every one is bespoke, and I don't consistently drive the findings doc into a proposal. Harvest converted by luck (their guy quit).

Break-fix → agreement: my worst fumble. I have the billing data proving three clients spent more on T&M last year than an agreement would cost, and I've never once run that comparison and shown it to them. If the software built that one-pager from my own invoices, that conversation stops being "selling" and becomes "reporting," which I can do all day.

Hardware quotes: I do them fine but slowly, and margin makes me feel like I'm cheating someone, so I chronically underprice. Compliance packages: strong offering, underpriced, no defined SKU — every credit union gets a slightly different handmade thing.

## 8. What "teach me to grow" would honestly mean

I'm allergic to sales methodology content — the "10x your MRR" webinar crowd makes my skin crawl, and any pep talk in a sidebar gets muted immediately. I'd also be skeptical that a methodology built for a 50-person MSP with a sales rep maps onto a company that is one anxious person. And skeptical that AI drafts sound like me instead of like a sequence.

Honestly, "teach" would mean: use *my* numbers to make the case ("you close 80% of assessments — you've proposed zero this quarter"), tell me the one specific action on one specific deal, make pricing less naked (even rough peer benchmarks so $1,450 doesn't come purely from fear), and — the real one — notice when I'm avoiding, and lower the cost of doing the thing anyway. I don't need software to teach me sales theory. I need it to be the colleague who says "Dana, it's day nine on Pinewood, here's the draft, hit send." I know exactly what I should be doing. I need something that makes not-doing-it harder than doing it.
