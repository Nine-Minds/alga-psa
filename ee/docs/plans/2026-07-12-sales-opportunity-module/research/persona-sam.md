# Persona interview: Sam Delgado — 25-year veteran MSP owner

> Design-research artifact for the sales opportunity module. Simulated persona interview, 2026-07-12.
> Persona: 25 years in, 25 techs across 3 offices in a tri-city metro, ~$6.5M revenue, ~70% recurring. One full-time salesperson, two account managers doing QBRs; owner still closes the big deals. 15 years on ConnectWise; abandoned Salesforce ($40k+ wasted). Evaluating AlgaPSA as a replacement.

## 1. The sales machine today

Lead sources, in order: referrals from existing clients (about 60% of new logos, and our CPA and law firm clients are the best referrers), my peer group and local manufacturing association, vendor channel reps tossing us deals they can't service, and a trickle from the website that's mostly tire-kickers. Who touches what: Dana, my salesperson, qualifies and runs deals under about $4k MRR. Anything bigger, I'm in the room, because after 25 years the relationships are mine. Trish and Kevin, my AMs, own existing accounts and theoretically feed expansion pipeline. The truth about the pipeline lives in three places: a spreadsheet called Pipeline_Master_v7_FINAL(2).xlsx that Dana updates Sunday nights, Dana's personal Notion doc where the *real* notes are, and my head. ConnectWise has opportunity records, but they're carcasses - created to generate a quote, never touched again. Where it lies to me: close dates that roll forward every month like a mirage, stages that describe the last activity instead of the client's actual commitment, and deal values that include hardware nobody's committed to. Recent big win: 85-seat law firm, referred by a CPA client, 14 months of courtship, closed at $14,200/mo plus about $60k in onboarding and hardware. That one never lived in any system - it lived in my calendar and gut. Recent embarrassment: 120-endpoint manufacturer, sat in our pipeline at 80% probability, "closing this month" for four consecutive months. Kevin ran into their controller at a Rotary lunch and learned they'd signed with a competitor six weeks earlier. Our own pipeline was forecasting revenue from a corpse.

## 2. Forecasting

Honestly? Revenue planning is: recurring base, minus a 5% churn assumption I made up in 2019, plus "Dana should close $60k of new MRR this year," plus whatever I personally feel good about. The weighted-pipeline number CW can produce is fiction and everyone knows it. Stage percentages are broken because they multiply two lies: a stage that reflects seller optimism, and a percentage some consultant made up. 50% of a $200k deal is not $100k - that deal closes or it doesn't. And I sandbag hard, because in 2016 I hired two techs against an optimistic forecast and ate six months of payroll on deals that evaporated. What I'd trust: probability derived from *verifiable prospect actions*, not rep opinion. Did they sign the assessment agreement? Give us network credentials? Put the decision-maker in a meeting? Did we send a contract? Those are facts. Second thing I'd trust: per-seller calibration from history. If Dana's "90% deals" close 40% of the time, show me that and adjust. Give me a band - floor of signed-plus-verbal, ceiling of everything with real evidence - not one falsely precise number.

## 3. Sales-to-onboarding handoff

The one that still stings: 60-seat medical practice, three years ago. Dana promised, verbally, that email migration was included, that their EHR vendor's remote-access setup would carry over untouched, and that go-live would land before their compliance audit. None of it made it into the quote or the project. My onboarding engineer discovered all three on day one. We ate roughly 40 unbilled hours, blew the audit date, and the office manager spent the first 90 days convinced we were liars. They stayed, but they refused the backup and security add-ons for two years - trust was spent. What gets lost in every handoff: verbal promises, pricing exceptions, the stakeholder map (who's the champion, who hates change, who really signs), and technical discovery that was done during sales and then re-done, billably awkwardly, during onboarding. CRM-plus-PSA duct tape loses it because the handoff is a human re-typing a summary. A PSA that owns both sides should make the opportunity *become* the onboarding project: every promise is a line item or task the moment it's spoken, the quote lines map directly to agreement lines and project tasks, and the engineer sees the whole courtship, not a two-line handoff note.

## 4. QBRs and account management

Trish and Kevin run quarterly reviews for our top 40 accounts. Each deck takes three hours to assemble from CW reports, and the meeting is entirely backward-looking: ticket counts, SLA stats, "any concerns?" The client nods, everyone feels professional, zero pipeline gets created. Why: my AMs are relationship people, not hunters; nothing in front of them screams "opportunity"; and logging an upsell in CW is enough friction that they just... don't. What they need shoved in their face, pre-built, per account: contract renewal dates at 120 days out, assets past warranty or five years old, OS end-of-life counts, ticket volume trending up per user, seat drift (we're billing 45, agent count says 52 - that's real money leaking), and a whitespace grid - here are our nine service lines, here are the four this client doesn't buy. Give me that grid across all clients and I'd find $30k of MRR in an afternoon. The QBR should end with the system asking: "Three triggers fired. Create opportunities?"

## 5. Multi-seller discipline

What CW and Salesforce both got wrong: the system took data and gave nothing back. Salesforce, after $40k of consultant, was 22 required fields per opportunity so my dashboard would look pretty. Salespeople correctly identified it as surveillance plus a data-entry tax and quietly starved it. The only thing that has *ever* worked for me is two-part: first, the Monday pipeline meeting is run live from the system on the big screen - if it's not in there, it doesn't exist, and I mean that literally because part two is commission is calculated *only* from what's in the system. No record, no check. That got Dana honest inside a month. The system can help by doing the work: auto-log the emails and meetings, pre-draft the update, make touching a deal a 20-second act. But the teeth are money and the meeting.

## 6. Agreement attach and deal shape

New logo motion: paid assessment at $2,500 (credited back if they sign), onboarding project at $150-250/seat, then the agreement at $125-175/user/month depending on stack. The assessment is the whole game - close rate after a paid assessment is triple. T&M-to-contract: I want my hourly clients ranked by trailing-12-month spend; anyone over ~$1,200/month average should be getting a conversion pitch, and nobody systematically does it. My two junior sellers back in the day attached agreements to new logos at 55% and 85% respectively, and I only figured that out a year too late from billing data. Hardware is 12-18% margin glue, not a business - but it's noise polluting my pipeline numbers. Renewals: I want a renewal pipeline auto-built at T-120 with escalator discipline, because Kevin lets clients slide on the 4% increase and Trish doesn't. Across three offices I want: attach rate per seller, average MRR per new logo, assessment-to-agreement conversion, realized effective rate vs quoted, and agreement margin by office - I'm fairly sure my north office underprices and I can't prove it.

## 7. Serious vs. toy

I take it seriously if the opportunity is *natively* wired to the things AlgaPSA already has: quote lines that become agreement lines that become the onboarding project. And MRR versus one-time as first-class citizens - a $200k deal that's $3k MRR is a completely different animal from $150k of hardware, and CW's pipeline shows one dumb number. Minimum to beat the spreadsheet: updating a deal in under 30 seconds, MRR/NRR split, evidence-based stages, auto-generated renewal pipeline, and it lives where the email already is. The CW failure they should be terrified of: shipping a module that *exists* but is a bolted-on generic CRM skeleton - opportunities that don't become agreements, forecast reports nobody trusts, screens so slow reps refuse. A checkbox module is worse than nothing, because it lets you claim the box is checked while the spreadsheet quietly stays king.

## 8. Automation

Where I'd let software act: flag any deal with 14 days of silence to the rep, escalate to me at 21; auto-create renewal opportunities at T-120; auto-build the QBR trigger list and whitespace grid; auto-draft follow-up emails *for the rep to send*; auto-log every email and meeting so the record builds itself; auto-downgrade probability when the evidence isn't there and say why. Where it would destroy trust: anything auto-*sent* to a client - one robotic "just checking in!" to that law firm's managing partner and I lose a $170k/year relationship. No auto-pricing without human eyes, ever. And internally: nudge the rep privately before embarrassing them on my dashboard. Software should be the honest bookkeeper and the tireless assistant. The moment it starts talking to my clients or ambushing my people, it's fired - and I've fired better.
